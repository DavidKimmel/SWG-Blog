Chapter Two

Faith &
Deeds

"Show me your faith apart from your works, and I will show you my faith by my works." — James 2:18

James 2:1–26

No Favoritism

James opens this chapter with a confronting scenario: a wealthy person enters the assembly adorned in fine clothes and receives the best seat, while a poor person is told to stand aside or sit on the floor. This is not merely bad manners — James calls it evil. To show favoritism is to become a judge with corrupt motives, violating the royal law that commands us to love our neighbors as ourselves.

"My brothers and sisters, believers in our glorious Lord Jesus Christ must not show favoritism." — James 2:1 NIV

Faith Without Works Is Dead

The second half of the chapter addresses a central theme of the letter: genuine faith is always productive. If a brother or sister is without food or clothing and you offer them warm words but no practical help, what good is it? Faith that exists only as intellectual agreement — even the demons believe and shudder — is no faith at all.

"For as the body apart from the spirit is dead, so also faith apart from works is dead." — James 2:26 ESV

Abraham and Rahab

James anchors his argument with two unlikely examples. Abraham, the father of faith, was justified not by belief alone but by the act of offering his son Isaac — his faith was made complete by what he did. And Rahab, a Canaanite prostitute, was justified because she acted on her belief by sheltering the Israelite spies. In both cases, deeds were not additions to faith; they were faith made visible.
