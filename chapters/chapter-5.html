The Finish Line

A Study of James Chapter 5

Every letter has to end somewhere. Most of the New Testament epistles close with warm greetings, travel plans, a benediction, the kind of gentle landing that eases the reader back into ordinary life. Jacob does not do that.

He ends the way he began — with urgency, with pastoral fire, with the voice of a man who has seen too much to soften what needs to be said. His final chapter moves through a prophet's warning, a farmer's patience, a sick man's prayer, and a wanderer being brought home. It is not a neat conclusion. It is a commissioning.

Jacob is not trying to wrap things up. He is sending you back out.

A Prophet's Roar

Verses 1–6

The opening of chapter 5 is among the most startling passages in the New Testament. Jacob turns his attention to the wealthy — not the wealthy within the community, but the powerful elite who have been exploiting his readers — and what follows sounds less like a pastoral letter and more like Amos or Isaiah in full cry.

Weep and howl for the miseries that are coming upon you.

He catalogues their sins with precision. Their wealth is rotting. Their fine garments are moth-eaten. Their gold and silver have corroded — and that corrosion, Jacob says, will be a witness against them, eating their flesh like fire. They have hoarded treasure in the last days, accumulating wealth while the world around them suffers, apparently convinced that the stockpile is security.

Then comes the charge that carries the most weight: they have kept back the wages of the workers who harvested their fields. In the ancient world, a day laborer depended on that day's wages for that day's food. Withholding it was not a financial transaction — it was an act of violence against people who had no margin, no cushion, no recourse. Jacob does not treat it as a business matter. He treats it as a cry ascending to heaven.

And here he introduces a title for God that appears nowhere else in the New Testament: the Lord of Sabaoth. Not a reference to the Sabbath — this is a military title. The Commander of Heavenly Armies. The God who marshals the hosts of heaven is the one who has heard the cries of the unpaid harvesters. The implication is unmistakable: what has been done in the fields will be answered in the courts of a Judge whose power is beyond appeal.

Jacob's words here are not primarily a call to political action, though they carry political implications. They are a reminder that the marginalized are not invisible. Their suffering does not disappear into the indifference of a silent universe. It rises. It is heard. And the one who hears it is not without power to act.

Pause and reflect: The Lord of Sabaoth hears the cries of the exploited. How does that reality change the way you think about injustice you witness — whether in your community, your workplace, or the broader world?

The Farmer's Wisdom

Verses 7–11

Having delivered his prophetic warning, Jacob turns to his suffering readers — the ones on the receiving end of the exploitation — and offers them something they need more than anger. He offers them a framework for endurance.

He holds up two models, and both are worth looking at carefully.

The farmer. He has planted. He has done everything within his power to do. And now he waits — for the early rains, for the late rains, for the soil to do what soil does in its own time. He cannot hurry the harvest. He cannot manufacture the rain. What he can do is establish his heart — settle into patient trust, refuse to be destabilized by the waiting — knowing that the rains will come in their season.

Jacob tells his readers to do the same. The coming of the Lord is near. Justice will arrive. The arc of history is not random. But the timing belongs to God, and the farmer's job is not to control the weather — it is to hold steady until it breaks.

Then he introduces a word that operates differently from the hypomonē we have seen throughout the letter. He calls for makrothumia — long-temperedness, the specific quality of patience that operates not in relation to circumstances but in relation to people. Hypomonē is what you need when the trial is heavy. Makrothumia is what you need when the person in front of you is difficult — when you are tempted to explode, to retaliate, to give up on someone who has hurt you or worn you out. It is the slow-burning patience of a person who has decided not to give their anger the final word.

Job. Jacob holds him up as the example of hypomonē, and it is worth pausing on this, because Job is not an obvious candidate. Job did not suffer quietly. He argued with God, demanded answers, sat in the ashes and refused easy comfort. His staying power was not the absence of anguish — it was the refusal to quit. He stayed in the conversation, stayed in the relationship, stayed in the struggle. And in the end, Jacob says, he saw the purpose of the Lord — that at the center of the story was a God full of compassion and mercy.

The trials you are in have a purpose. You may not see it yet. Job didn't, for a long time. But the one who allowed the trial is the same one who is compassionate and merciful. Those two things are not in tension. They belong to the same God.

Let Your Yes Be Yes

Verse 12

It would be easy to read verse 12 as a throwaway — a brief detour on the way to the prayer section. It is not. Jacob is returning, one final time, to the theme of integrity that has run beneath everything in this letter.

Let your yes be yes and your no be no.

In first-century Jewish culture, elaborate oaths had become a kind of verbal currency — ways of signaling that this time you really meant it, that this particular commitment was more binding than your ordinary word. The problem, of course, is that once you need an oath to be believed, you have already admitted that your plain word is not reliable.

Jacob traces this back to dipsychos — the double-mindedness that has been the letter's recurring diagnosis. A person who is genuinely whole, genuinely integrated, has no need for verbal gymnastics. Their yes means yes because they are the kind of person whose word can be trusted. Their character is consistent enough that the oath is redundant.

This is a small verse with a large implication: the quality of your speech in the small moments — your follow-through on ordinary commitments, your honesty in low-stakes conversations — reveals more about the state of your soul than almost anything else.

The Power of Prayer

Verses 13–18

Jacob now arrives at what may be the most personally revealing section of the entire letter. This is the man called Camel Knees writing about prayer — and it shows. He does not describe prayer as a theological concept. He describes it as the living tissue that holds community together through suffering, sickness, sin, and restoration.

The structure he outlines is communal, not merely individual. When someone is suffering, they pray. When someone is cheerful, they sing praises. When someone is sick, they call for the elders — the recognized spiritual leaders of the community — who come, anoint with oil in the name of the Lord, and pray over them. The prayer of faith, Jacob says, will restore the sick person. And if there is sin involved, it will be forgiven.

Confess your sins to one another and pray for one another, that you may be healed.

This verse has carried centuries of theological debate, and the debates are worth having. But underneath the theological questions is a pastoral reality that Jacob understood from experience: there is something that happens in the act of honest confession to another human being that cannot happen in the privacy of your own heart alone. Secrecy feeds what it hides. Confession begins to dismantle it. And the community that can hold each other's confessions with grace and pray for one another with honesty is a community experiencing something close to what Jacob means by wholeness.

Then he introduces Elijah — and the way he introduces him is deliberate. Elijah was a man with a nature like ours. He was not a superhuman. He was not operating on a level of spiritual attainment unavailable to ordinary believers. He was, Jacob says, just like us. Prone to fear, prone to despair, capable of sitting under a broom tree and asking God to let him die. And yet — his fervent prayer shut the sky for three years and six months, and his prayer opened it again.

The power, Jacob is insisting, is not in the person praying. It is in the Lord of Sabaoth who hears. The same Commander of Heavenly Armies who hears the cries of the oppressed harvesters also hears the prayers of ordinary, broken, Camel-Knees believers who come before him with honest need.

Key question: What is preventing you from bringing your most pressing need before the Commander of Heavenly Armies today? Elijah was just like you — and his prayers moved heaven.

The Final Commission

Verses 19–20

Jacob does not close with a benediction. He closes with a mission.

If anyone among you wanders from the truth and someone brings him back — let him know that whoever brings back a sinner from his wandering will save a soul from death and will cover a multitude of sins.

There is no formal ending. No farewell. Just this: go find the wanderer and bring them home.

This is not a task for specialists. Jacob does not say if the elders bring back a sinner or if the theologically trained bring back a sinner. He says if anyone among you — the full, ordinary, struggling, double-minded community of people who have been reading this letter and recognizing themselves in its pages. These are the people Jacob commissions.

The word he uses for wandering is the same word used throughout scripture for sheep that have strayed. The image is not of someone who has dramatically abandoned the faith in a moment of rebellion. It is the picture of someone who has drifted — one small step at a time, one compromise at a time, one season of difficulty at a time — until they look up and realize they are nowhere near where they started.

And the task of the community is to go after them. Not to condemn. Not to make them feel the full weight of how far they've wandered. To bring them back.

This is dikaiosynē — the relational justice that puts things right, that heals what is broken, that restores the person to the community and the community to wholeness. It is, Jacob suggests, among the most significant things one person can do for another.

Wholeness: The End of the Letter and the Beginning of the Life

Jacob began this letter with a man named Ya'akov who had once stood on the outside of faith, skeptical of his own brother, convinced the whole thing was either deluded or dangerous. He became, through a single encounter with the risen Christ, the man called Camel Knees — a pillar, a pastor, a prophet, a servant. He ends his letter not with his own story but with yours.

The entire letter has been a mirror. It has shown us our double-mindedness, our favoritism, our untamed tongues, our internal wars, our arrogant plans, our prayerlessness, our tendency to hear the Word and walk away unchanged. It has not been comfortable. Jacob did not intend it to be.

But the mirror is not there to condemn. It is there to show us where the fractures are so that the Father of Lights — the unchanging, generous, compassionate God who gives liberally and without finding fault — can do his slow and patient work of making us teleios. Whole. Complete. Lacking nothing.

Not sinlessly perfect. Integrated. The kind of people whose faith has finally caught up with their confession. The kind of people whose words and actions tell the same story. The kind of people who, like Elijah, are nothing extraordinary in themselves — but who have learned to pray to an extraordinary God.

The letter ends. The life it describes is just beginning.

For reflection as you close this study:

Are you in a season requiring hypomonē — endurance under the weight of circumstances — or makrothumia — long-temperedness toward a difficult person? How does knowing God's purpose is compassionate and merciful help you stay the course?

Is there a wanderer in your life — someone who has drifted from the truth, one small step at a time — whom you are being called to go after?

Looking back across the whole letter: what has the mirror shown you? Where is the most significant gap between what you believe and how you live? Bring that honestly before God.

Lord of Sabaoth — Commander of Heavenly Armies, Father of Lights, the God who hears the cry of the suffering and the prayer of the ordinary — make me whole. Not perfect, but integrated. Not sinless, but consistent. Let my yes mean yes. Let my faith have hands. Let my words bring life. And when I see the wanderer, give me the courage to go. Amen.
"Whoever brings back a sinner from his wandering will save a soul from death and will cover a multitude of sins." — Jacob, brother of Jesus, pillar of the church, servant of God
