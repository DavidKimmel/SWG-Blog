Chapter Five

Patient
Endurance

"Be patient, therefore, brothers, until the coming of the Lord." — James 5:7

James 5:1–20

Warning to the Rich

James opens this final chapter with perhaps his most severe passage — a prophetic condemnation of the wealthy who have hoarded treasure, withheld wages from workers, and lived in luxury while the innocent suffered. The rust on their gold and silver will testify against them. This is not an attack on wealth itself but on wealth that has become an idol, a source of injustice, and a substitute for trust in God.

"You have laid up treasure in the last days. Behold, the wages of the laborers who mowed your fields, which you kept back by fraud, are crying out against you." — James 5:3-4 ESV

The Patience of Job

Against that backdrop of injustice, James calls the suffering to patience — not passive resignation, but the active, anchored waiting of a farmer who plants seed and trusts the autumn and spring rains will come. He points to Job as a model: despite profound suffering, Job persisted, and saw the Lord's compassion and mercy in the end. The coming of the Lord is the horizon that makes present suffering bearable.

"You have heard of the steadfastness of Job, and you have seen the purpose of the Lord, how the Lord is compassionate and merciful." — James 5:11 ESV

The Power of Prayer

James closes the letter on a note of profound communal care. The elders of the church are to pray over the sick, anointing with oil in the name of the Lord. Confess your sins to one another and pray for one another that you may be healed. He recalls Elijah — a man with a nature like ours — who prayed, and the rain stopped, and prayed again, and the rain fell. And finally, the greatest act of endurance: turning back a wanderer from the error of their way saves a soul from death and covers a multitude of sins.
