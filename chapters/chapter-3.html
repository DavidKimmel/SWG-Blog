Chapter Three

Taming the
Tongue

"For every kind of beast and bird... can be tamed... but no human being can tame the tongue." — James 3:7-8

James 3:1–18

The Weight of Teaching

James begins with a sober warning: not many should become teachers, because those who teach will be held to a stricter standard of judgment. This is not a discouragement from teaching, but a call to take the responsibility seriously. The tongue steers the whole person like a bit in a horse's mouth or a rudder on a great ship — small, but capable of determining the entire course.

"If anyone does not stumble in what he says, he is a perfect man, able also to bridle his whole body." — James 3:2 ESV

A Fire That Defiles

The imagery James employs is vivid and alarming. The tongue is a fire — a world of unrighteousness set among our members, staining the whole body and setting the entire course of life ablaze. Wild animals of every kind have been domesticated by human hands, yet no one can fully tame the tongue. From the same mouth come blessing and cursing — and this ought not to be so.

"With it we bless our Lord and Father, and with it we curse people who are made in the likeness of God." — James 3:9 ESV

Wisdom From Above

The chapter closes with a contrast between two kinds of wisdom. Earthly wisdom is characterized by bitter jealousy and selfish ambition — it is unspiritual, even demonic, and produces disorder and every vile practice. The wisdom that comes from above is pure, peaceable, gentle, open to reason, full of mercy and good fruits. The harvest of righteousness is sown in peace by those who make peace.
